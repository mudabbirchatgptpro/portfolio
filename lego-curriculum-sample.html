Is It Alive? Teaching an AI to Tell

A hands-on K-8 Computer Science & AI unit · Grades 4-6 · 5 sessions of 45 minutes
I wrote this sample to show how I design a build-based, standards-aligned unit: how a young learner uncovers what machine learning actually is by training a classifier with their own hands, no code required. It is illustrative, written for this application, not a published LEGO Education product.

The big idea

Students act as the teacher of a simple image classifier. By collecting their own examples, testing the model, watching it fail, and fixing it, they discover the core truth of machine learning: a model only knows what it has been shown, and the human decides what "good" looks like. The unit ends with each student writing one rule for using AI responsibly, drawn from something their own model got wrong.

Standards alignment

CSTA 1B-AP-09 (models & simulations) CSTA 1B-IC-18 (computing & society) ISTE 1.5 Computational Thinker ISTE 1.3 Knowledge Constructor NGSS SEP-6 (constructing explanations) NGSS SEP-4 (analyzing & interpreting data)

These are the frameworks the unit is built to. NGSS Science and Engineering Practices carry the "collect data, test, revise" spine; CSTA and ISTE carry the computing and computational-thinking outcomes.

Learning objectives

By the end, a student can:

  • Explain, in their own words, that a model learns from examples a person chooses.
  • Improve a model's accuracy by changing its training data, not its "settings."
  • Identify one case their model gets wrong and explain why, using evidence.
  • State one responsible-use rule that follows from that failure.

The five sessions

Session 1 · 45 min
Sorting without rules
Students hand-sort a pile of objects (or picture cards) into two groups, then try to write the exact rule they used. They discover the rule is hard to pin down, which sets up why we would want a machine to learn from examples instead.
Session 2 · 45 min
Training the machine
Using a no-code image trainer, teams collect and label their own examples for two categories and train a classifier. First contact with "the model learns from what I give it."
Session 3 · 45 min
Breaking it on purpose
Teams try to fool their own model and log every failure. The engineering practice here is deliberate: predict, test, observe, record.
Session 4 · 45 min
Fixing it with data
Teams add examples to fix their failures and re-test, charting accuracy before and after. The insight lands: you improve the model by improving its data.
Session 5 · 45 min
One honest rule
Each student writes a single responsible-use rule tied to a real failure their model had, and the class builds a shared "how we use AI" wall.

Differentiation & access

Emerging readers
Picture-card sorting and spoken explanations replace written logs; sentence stems for the responsible-use rule.
Stretch
Teams introduce a deliberately biased training set and predict the failure before testing it.
Multilingual learners
Category labels in home language first; the build-and-test loop carries meaning without heavy text.

Why this shape

Every session is a build-test-revise loop, because the fastest way to understand a model is to be the one training it and watching it fail. The abstract idea, that AI reflects the data and the values behind it, arrives through the student's own hands, which is how confidence and creativity actually form. That is the pedagogy I would bring to a K-8 Computer Science and AI curriculum.
