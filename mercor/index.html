Mercor's data quality comes down to one thing: whether thousands of experts, following the same rubric, score the same answer the same way. When they do, the model learns a clean signal. When they do not, it learns noise. My job is to write the instruction that gets them to agree. Below is a live piece of that idea you can try, then things I have built.

Why the rubric is the whole job

You are one of several experts grading an AI answer for a training set. Score it twice, once on a vague rubric and once on a sharp one, and watch what happens to the agreement between reviewers.

User: What are the main terms of the 30-year fixed mortgage you recommend?
AI answer: This is a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage at an APR of 6.9%. The monthly payment covers principal and interest, while property taxes and homeowner's insurance are collected separately through an escrow account.
Reviewer agreement0%
That gap between the first rubric and the second is where a Learning Designer lives. Multiply it across 30,000 experts and it is the difference between data a lab keeps and data it sends back. Writing the guideline, the rubric, and the check that gets reviewers to agree, then certifying that they can, is the work I want to do here.
The other experts' scores here are illustrative, but the effect is real: inter-rater agreement is what turns human judgment into a training signal a model can use.

Selected work

Data labeling and evaluation pipeline

Independent build · rubric-driven annotation at scale

Problem: a labeling specification that reads clearly to its author still produces inconsistent labels once non-specialists and AI agents run it at volume.

Built: a two-pass system that turns an annotation spec into step-by-step instructions workers and agents apply consistently to label and evaluate documents, with a second review pass that catches where the spec quietly breaks down.

Why it maps: this is your project-level instructional design in miniature, the exact loop of writing guidelines, finding where they fail, and tightening them until the output is reliable.

Instructional playbooks across a six-country network

Beaconhouse School System · 380,000+ students, 1,500+ staff

Problem: toolkits that worked at one flagship campus fell apart when a teacher on another continent used them without the author in the room.

Built: standardized playbooks, facilitator guides, and assessment rubrics, pressure-tested until someone I had never met produced the right result from them, then deployed across campuses in six countries.

Why it maps: the same test your talent experts face, consistent performance from people following instructions cold, at the scale your academies need.

Adaptive tutoring engine with certification-style mastery

Independent build · learning science + full-stack

Problem: most training programs confirm that someone is familiar with a skill, not that they can actually do it well enough to be trusted with harder work.

Built: an engine with a Bayesian mastery model and item generation that keeps a learner at the edge of their ability and only advances them once the estimate says they have truly mastered a skill, not merely seen it.

Why it maps: this is the machinery behind assessments and certification that validate a talent expert is ready for a project type, rather than guessing. Try the mastery mechanic on the main portfolio.

Published research: preparing humans to oversee AI

Peer-reviewed · 100+ citations across 8 papers, h-index 5

What: a competency framework for human oversight of agentic AI workplaces, on exactly the question Mercor is built around, which parts of judgment stay human and how you train and verify them.

Why it maps: I do not just build the instruction, I study what makes human judgment reliable enough to teach a model. Publications on Scholar.
